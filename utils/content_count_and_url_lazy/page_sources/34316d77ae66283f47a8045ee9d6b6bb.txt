the force feedback for each of the variants has in turn been carefully fine-tuned for each car\u00b4s characteristics for a much more detailed feel for the revamped tire and suspension physics.\n\nThe whole physics development process both for creating new cars as well as revising existing cars begins with extensive data collection on the vehicle at hand - sometimes manufacturers will provide us with all we could need, and sometimes it\u00b4s on us to go on the hunt to scramble the required info from various sources - once we have collected at least the basic specs and dimensions (mass, engine output, gear ratios, positions of aero devices, wheelbase, track width), a baseline is created on top of an existing similar car (or the \"old\" car if it\u00b4s a revision instead of a new car), applying those basic specs to it.\n\n[b]Pictured Below: [\/b]Thorough Factory Setup sheet for Ginetta G58\n[img]{STEAM_CLAN_IMAGE}\/35138208\/ec4d167b2c44c2db9052dbfad1a9dc064e3d97a9.png[\/img]\n\nThe chassis then gets its undertray and collision mesh defined - this is important not only for defining the car\u00b4s \"hit box\" against walls and other cars, but also to set up the floor coordinates which is the reference plane from which many physics calculations are drawn from. The floor position and angle also affects how the car reacts when bottoming out or scrapping over curbs.\n\n[b]Pictured below: [\/b]Formula Dirt collision thing.\n\n[b]Pictured below:[\/b] The suspension geometry of a P1 prototype as provided by its constructor.\n[img]{STEAM_CLAN_IMAGE}\/35138208\/f6280132d43f076b4bd802345ba8d1af14f4c907.jpg[\/img]\n[img]{STEAM_CLAN_IMAGE}\/35138208\/a7dbe4d0481511e6f7c41ee8d0b49fe34a178040.jpg[\/img]\n\nFor
